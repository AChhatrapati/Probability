\textbf{Question 1.1.3)}Points $\vec{A},\vec{B},\vec{C}$ are defined to be collinear if
$$\text{rank}\myvec{1 & 1 & 1\\ \vec{A} & \vec{B} & \vec{C}}=2$$ 
Are the given points in (1.1) collinear?\\
\solution
Given,
\begin{align}
\vec{A} = \myvec{1\\-1};
\vec{B} = \myvec{-4\\6};
\vec{C} = \myvec{-3\\-5}\\
\myvec{1 & 1 & 1\\ \vec{A} & \vec{B} & \vec{c}}&=\myvec{1 & 1 & 1\\ 1 & -4 & -3 \\ -1 & 6 & -5}\\
\mydet{1 & 1 & 1\\ 1 & -4 & -3\\ -1 & 6 & -5}&=1\mydet{-4 & -3 \\ 6 & -5}-1\mydet{-3 & 1\\ -5 & -1}+1\mydet{1 & -4 \\ -1 & 6}\\
&=1\times38-1\times8+1\times2\\
&=32\neq0\\
\implies\text{rank}\myvec{1 & 1 & 1\\ \vec{A} & \vec{B} & \vec{C}}&=3
\end{align}
$\vec{A},\vec{B},\vec{C}$ are not collinear.
